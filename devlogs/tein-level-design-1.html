How to design a TEiN level

This is not so much a tutorial on how to use the actual tools, rather it's intended to give you some principles that will help with the actual design process.

Before we actually get into the design process, we'll go over a few basic rules that you should essentially always be following. These are the kind of rules that you need a very good reason to break, and if you're a beginner then any reason you may think you have is probably not actually a good enough.

  1. The player should always be safe when entering a level.

sometimes, it is nearly impossible for the level design to avoid potentially killing the player in the starting position. but even in such cases, one should give the player a sufficient amount of time to react to and avoid the thing that can kill them

Always leave about 3 tiles of margin between the edge of the screen and anything that can kill the player

don't use automatically falling movement tags. You almost invariably want to use the player-triggered falling movmenet tags and just have the falling trigger as soon as the player moves out of the starting zone

common design advice

this is the kind of advice you can apply to almost any game's design. I remember hearing this kind of advice all the time in Mario Maker.

use a small subset of the game's mechanics in each area It's very common for beginners to create levels or areas where they use almost every single entity in the game. This is probably because the player is much more likely to get at least a couple ideas about how to use all the various elements in the game, but they haven't yet spent enough time thinking deeply about each individual element to generate several ideas about how to use that one element.

your levels are always much easier for you then they are for everyone else

give space for your level design to breathe this is even more important in tein because the camera doesn't move you should not be packing so much stuff into a single level aim for roughly 10-30 seconds of gameplay on a screen if a single level takes more than 15 seconds to beat when played perfectly, it's probably too long

The design process

understand that the movement tags are the most interesting part of the game's design if your level does not make use of movement tags in some way, then it's probably a very boring level

making absurdly tight jumps and navigating snaking hallways of kukos is really not fun!

levels don't always need a gimmick. just having solid and interesting layouts is often good enough

It's very possible that my design process may sound a little too abstract, or be hard to seehow one puts it into practice.

So after giving an overview of my process I'll actually go through it as well to show how you can take something very abstract and make it into a real level.

I generally start by thinking about the motion that I want the level to have this can be some pattern of motion which objects int eh level will follow, or some intersting path for the player to traverse. At this stage, the idea is something extremely basic, just a couple of general shapes.

Focus on a couple basic shapes and then consider how those shapes can be composed in interesting permutations scale and rotate the shapes, flip them on each axis, consider how two or more shapes may intersect and remember that these shapes represent motion, where an object may be across some span of time if you have multiple objects moving along different paths, consider how the objects' relative phase will affect their interaction

for beginners, it's very natural to just sit down and start drawing a level very often just "filling space" if you are simply "filling space", then again, the levle design is probably not very intersting.

Now, there are times when you do need to just fill space in between the major elements of the level this is what I would refer to as the sort of "connective tissue" of a level

this connective tissue should be kept simple, and relatively easy compared to the level's main features. If the player is consistently dying in these sections of connective tissue, then they should be simplified further.

the method of thinking about levels in terms of these very simple shapes is a method which probably will only work for a very small subset of games You probably could not design mario bros levels in this way, at least not to the same degree. it is really particular to this game's design that this method works at all something to do with the very simple and compact nature of the game's level design

Personally, I have a historicaly been bad about feeling like every single level should do something completely original to surprise the player but this is something like letting the perfect bet he enemy of the good originality is not the be-all end-all the overall design of an area will benefit more from being a cohesive sequence of inter-related ideas If you come up with a fun system of moving parts, you should feel free to explore different arangements that highlight the interesting parts of this composition. player with the phase of the motion and change it fluidly into something else across a sequence of levels

If you have some sequence of smaler obstacles

because tein has such compact levels, you'll generally only be able to have 1-3 main obstacles per level.

connecting levels together

it's often a good idea to wait until you have a large collection of obstacles for an area before even finishing out individual levels This will give you more freedom to reorder or rearrange levels within an area can change where the exits on each level are located, sometimes this is easier to do if you have some margin in the level layout. another reason not to over-stuff your levels

it's better to have an area with 25 small levels than 15 levels that are too long and cramped

understanding what makes certain mechanics most interesting consider precisely what constraint the mechanic places on the player consider how a mechanic acts as a modifier on the underlying level geometry

gas clouds are a great example gas clouds are a sort of soft constraint both a sort of time constraint and a space constraint if you just have some hallway entirely covered in gas, then it's merely a 'do it faster' constraint which is not really that intersting most of the time you can also use gas clouds as a way of partitioning space into safe and unsafe regions this is more intersting than a simple time constraint, because it makes the player actually consider where they are going they may have to take a non-obvious route through the level simply due to the way the gas clouds parttion the space if you can come up with a layout that would require the player to play the level differently based solely on where the gas clouds are placed, that's interesting

when combining multiple different mechanics, consider how the constraints those mechanics place on the player can be played against one another

some constraints for the player to keep moving, others force the player to wait juxtaposing these creates tension the base game does this sort of thing in many places

collapsing motion can be either a waiting or hurry-up mechanism crumbling tiles force the player to hurry up, but with somevariability also a space constraint, as the breaking of a tile changes the levle layout player may need to think about the order in which they use crumbling tiles, treat as a sort of consumable resource mother is similar to crumbling tiles in a way, forces the player to keep moving, stay on their toes, can also be used to block off paths can force player to back off and come back after mother retreats e.g. player goes under low ceiling with mother, gets trapped into another space. unless player gets far enough away, they won't be able to get back out

weighted platforms can be used to force the player to stay grounded, if the player needs to pull down some piece of level geometry can easily play this against some element that tries to get the player to jump e.g. attach a gun to the same platform the player is trying to lower, or make the player navigate down through someguns anchored to the walls can also be used to keep the player jumping, depending on the surrounding level geometry can even swap back and forth if the other level geometry is changing dynamically

designing a level from layout first is the wrong way around start from things that are more primitive, then use layout or arrangement to facilitate the level's mechanics

reusing space

movement tags can bring in structures from offscreen essentially allowing for more vertical levels cyclic movement with wrap tags somewhat limited, but can create very interesting level designs buttons + locked doors it's a common trope to have certain layouts where the player needs to hit several buttons in order to open the exit door clever use of collapsing movement tags, revealing new paths as the player moves through the level
